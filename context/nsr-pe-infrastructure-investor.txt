Part I: Investment Approach & Thesis

Macquarie Asset Management — “The Infrastructure Industrialist”

Overview: World’s largest infrastructure manager (~$210bn in real assets AUM). Operates through regional
fund series: MIP (Americas), MEIF (Europe), MAIF (Asia-Paciﬁc). 30+ year track record, 55+ portfolio
companies, 26 realizations across Americas alone.

Digital infra thesis: Macquarie views digital infrastructure as essential, utility-like assets with
contracted/regulated revenue, inﬂation linkage, and structural demand growth. It invests across the full stack —
towers, ﬁbre, data centres — but with a strong preference for assets with operational improvement potential. The
Macquarie model is “buy, operate, optimize, exit”: acquire assets with operational complexity, apply active
management to improve margins and efﬁciency, then monetize through IPO, secondary sale, or dividend
recapture.

Key differentiators: (1) Deepest operational bench in the industry — Macquarie doesn’t just own infrastructure,
it runs it. TDC NET (Denmark), Vocus (Australia/NZ), and AirTrunk (sold to Blackstone at 8x entry valuation)
demonstrate hands-on management. (2) Fund diversiﬁcation by vintage, geography, and sector reduces
concentration risk. (3) Sustainability-linked ﬁnancing: TDC NET’s EUR 3bn in sustainability-linked bonds is the
template. (4) Longest track record of any infrastructure manager.

Hold periods: 7–12 years typical for core infrastructure; shorter (5–7 years) for value-add opportunities.

Target returns: Net 8–12% IRR for core/core+ infrastructure. Macquarie itself states returns of ~9.5% long-
term average; projected 8.3–11.0% over the next decade depending on macro scenario. Revenue growth
contributes 86% of EBITDA growth — leverage and multiple expansion are secondary.

Relevant portfolio: TDC NET (Denmark, full ownership), Vocus (Australia/NZ ﬁbre), SwyftFiber (US ﬁbre),
Diamond Infrastructure (US), AirTrunk (sold 2024 at ~$24bn AUD — approximately 8x entry valuation from
$3bn in 2020).

OMERS Infrastructure — “The Pension Fund Steward”

Overview: Infrastructure arm of OMERS, a C$140bn+ Canadian pension fund serving 640,000 Ontario public
sector workers. ~$28bn in infrastructure assets. 25+ year infrastructure investment history. Operates from
Toronto, London, Amsterdam, Singapore, Sydney.

Digital infra thesis: OMERS approaches digital infrastructure through the pension fund lens: ultra-long duration
(20–30 year hold horizons), liability-matching returns, and inﬂation protection. Digital infrastructure is attractive
because it combines the essential-service characteristics of traditional infrastructure (roads, utilities) with the
structural growth of technology adoption. OMERS is a patient capital provider — it doesn’t need to exit within a
fund lifecycle. This enables it to invest in assets during the capital-intensive build phase and harvest returns over
decades.

Key differentiators: (1) Permanent capital — no fund lifecycle pressure to exit. This fundamentally changes
investment behavior: OMERS can hold through downturns, reinvest in assets during construction phases, and
accept lower annual returns in exchange for compounding over longer periods. (2) Co-investment model:
OMERS frequently co-invests alongside other infrastructure funds, sharing risk while maintaining inﬂuence. (3)
Active stewardship: like Macquarie, OMERS takes operational roles in portfolio companies, not passive LP
positions. (4) Strong ESG/sustainability integration — 65% reduction in portfolio carbon emissions since 2019.

Hold periods: Effectively permanent for core assets. OMERS has held Bruce Power (energy) since 2003 (22+
years). Digital infrastructure holds are expected to be 10–20+ years.

Target returns: Plan-level target of ~7% net return (set annually as benchmark). Infrastructure contributes
steady mid-to-high single digit returns. OMERS reported 8.1% average annual net return over 2021–2024.
Infrastructure speciﬁcally “continues to deliver steady results, with most assets performing in line with
expectations.”

Relevant portfolio: XpFibre (France — largest independent FTTH operator, ~25% of French territory, raised
€5.8bn in credit facilities in 2024), Beanﬁeld Technologies (Canada — ﬁbre network), stakes in various digital
infrastructure across Europe and APAC.

KKR Global Infrastructure — “The Digital Ecosystem Builder”

Overview: KKR’s Global Infrastructure Strategy has committed ~$34bn of equity to digital infrastructure across
24 investments, plus $20bn+ in power/renewables. KKR is the single largest PE-style investor in digital
infrastructure globally. Infrastructure is managed alongside KKR’s broader PE, credit, and real estate platforms,
enabling cross-strategy deal origination and ﬁnancing.

Digital infra thesis: KKR takes a full-ecosystem approach: it invests simultaneously across the layers that
enable digital connectivity — ﬁbre networks, wireless towers, data centres, and the power infrastructure that
feeds them. KKR’s thesis is that AI and cloud computing are driving a structural supercycle in digital
infrastructure demand, and that the ﬁrms which control the physical assets enabling this demand will capture
outsized returns. Unlike Macquarie (operational optimizer) or OMERS (pension steward), KKR applies classic
PE value creation levers — operational improvement, strategic M&A, platform buildouts, and ﬁnancial
engineering — but on infrastructure-grade assets with more predictable cash ﬂows.

Key differentiators: (1) Largest digital infrastructure commitment of any single investor (~$34bn equity
deployed). (2) Platform-building strategy: KKR creates platforms from scratch (GTR data centres in Europe,
Open Dutch Fiber in Netherlands) and then scales them through organic growth and bolt-on acquisitions. (3)
Complex carve-out expertise: the TIM NetCo transaction (€22bn, the largest telecom infrastructure deal in
European history) demonstrates KKR’s ability to execute structurally complex deals that other investors cannot.
(4) Cross-strategy synergies: KKR’s credit arm can provide ﬁnancing for its infrastructure deals, and its PE
relationships provide deal ﬂow.

Hold periods: 5–10 years for value-add/growth infrastructure; longer for core assets that become platform plays.

Target returns: KKR targets mid-teens net IRR for infrastructure (higher than Macquarie/OMERS, reﬂecting
greater use of operational leverage and ﬁnancial engineering). For core digital infrastructure, returns are closer to
10–14%.

Relevant portfolio: FiberCop/NetCo (Italy — TIM’s former ﬁxed network, €22bn, serving 16m households),
Open Dutch Fiber (Netherlands), Reintel (Spain — ﬁbre), Global Technical Realty (Europe — data centres,
$2.5bn+ committed), 130,000+ wireless tower sites globally, 12 ﬁbre networks reaching ~30m households
globally, 5 data centre platforms across US/APAC/EMEA with 155+ facilities and a 12GW development
pipeline.

DigitalBridge — “The Pure-Play Digital Specialist”

Overview: The world’s only large-scale alternative asset manager 100% dedicated to digital infrastructure.
~$115bn AUM (as of December 2025). Listed on NYSE (DBRG). Founded by Marc Ganzi with 30+ years of
digital infrastructure investing and operating heritage. Headquartered in Boca Raton, with ofﬁces across North
America, Europe, Middle East, and Asia.

Digital infra thesis: DigitalBridge’s thesis is that digital infrastructure is not a subset of infrastructure — it is a
distinct asset class requiring specialized operating expertise that generalist infrastructure funds cannot replicate.
The ﬁrm invests across the “full stack” of digital infrastructure: macro cell towers, data centres (hyperscale,
colocation, edge), ﬁbre networks, small cells, and increasingly digital power. DigitalBridge’s edge is its operator
DNA: it doesn’t just provide capital, it provides management teams, operational playbooks, and strategic
direction. The ﬁrm’s model is to back or create platform companies, scale them through organic growth and
M&A, and then monetize through public listings, recapitalizations, or trade sales.

Key differentiators: (1) 100% digital focus — no dilution across transport, energy, or social infrastructure.
Every dollar of LP capital goes into digital. (2) Operator-investor hybrid: DigitalBridge portfolio companies are
actively managed, not passively held. The ﬁrm’s bench of digital infrastructure executives (many ex-operators
from American Tower, Equinix, CyrusOne) is unmatched. (3) Scale and deal ﬂow: $115bn AUM gives
DigitalBridge proprietary access to large-cap deals that smaller digital-focused funds cannot access. (4)
Expanding platform: recently added digital credit, liquid securities, and software strategies alongside the core
equity funds.

Hold periods: 4–7 years for value-add (DBP fund series); longer for core/core+ strategies.

Target returns: Mid-to-high-teens net IRR for the ﬂagship DBP fund series. DBP III closed at $11.7bn total
commitments (November 2025), with 65%+ from returning investors — the strongest validation of realized
returns.

Relevant portfolio: Vantage Data Centers (global hyperscale — $25bn Frontier campus in Texas), DataBank
(US edge/colo), Yondr Group (European DC developer), Scala Data Centers (Latin America), Vertical Bridge
(US towers — largest private tower company in the US), Zayo Group (US/Europe ﬁbre), JTOWER (Japan
towers), FiberNow (US ﬁbre). Global footprint spans 40+ countries.

Conference relevance: DigitalBridge is not on the NSR/BCG speaker list, but it is arguably the most
consequential capital allocator in the room’s sector. Any hallway conversation about tower valuations (Cellnex
session), data centre demand (AI theme), or ﬁbre consolidation (UK panel) is implicitly a conversation about
DigitalBridge’s market inﬂuence.

Brookﬁeld Infrastructure — “The Asset Recycler”

Overview: Infrastructure arm of Brookﬁeld Asset Management, which manages $1tn+ in total AUM. Brookﬁeld
Infrastructure Partners (NYSE: BIP) is a publicly listed infrastructure vehicle. Brookﬁeld’s infrastructure
franchise has raised ~$104bn in infrastructure equity funds over 2020–2024, making it the #1 ranked
infrastructure manager by capital raised (Infrastructure Investor 100, 2025).

Digital infra thesis: Brookﬁeld approaches digital infrastructure through its signature “acquire, improve,
recycle” model: buy high-quality infrastructure assets at attractive valuations, enhance operations to boost cash
ﬂow, and then recycle capital through partial or full exits to fund new investments. Brookﬁeld views data as a
core infrastructure vertical alongside energy, transport, and utilities. Its digital portfolio has grown rapidly — the
data segment generated FFO of $138m in Q3 2025, a 62% YoY increase — driven by tower acquisitions in India,
hyperscale data centre development, and organic growth across platforms.

Stonepeak — “The Thematic Deep-Diver”

Overview: A leading US-based alternative investment ﬁrm specializing in infrastructure and real assets with
~$73bn AUM. Founded in 2011 by Michael Dorrell and James Wyper (both ex-Blackstone infrastructure).
Stonepeak has invested in 20 digital infrastructure platforms across 40+ countries. Raised $48.6bn in
infrastructure funds over 2020–2024, ranking #7 globally (Infrastructure Investor 100, 2025).

Digital infra thesis: Stonepeak was an early mover in digital infrastructure, developing thematic investment
views ahead of the market. Its approach is deep-dive thematic: the ﬁrm evaluates 70+ opportunities per thesis,
selects the best-positioned platform, and then scales it aggressively through organic growth and bolt-ons.
Stonepeak’s focus on “irreplicable assets in fast-growing markets serving essential needs” leads it to invest in
interconnection-rich data centres (where network density creates moats), critical subsea/long-haul ﬁbre, and
tower portfolios in structurally undersupplied markets.

Key differentiators: (1) Early mover advantage: Stonepeak’s 2017 investment in Cologix (carrier-neutral
interconnection data centres) preceded the AI-driven DC boom by 6 years, capturing value at lower entry
multiples. (2) Multi-platform DC strategy: portfolio now includes Cologix, CoreSite, Digital Edge (Asia), Cirion
(LatAm), and the newly launched Montera Infrastructure ($1.5bn equity commitment for US hyperscale AI data
centres). Combined: 100+ facilities, 500+ MW operational, 400+ MW pipeline. (3) Nordic exposure: Stonepeak
owns a 49% stake in Cellnex Nordics (Sweden and Denmark, 5,200+ sites) — directly relevant to the
conference’s Nordic dimension and to the Cellnex session. (4) Defensive positioning: Stonepeak emphasizes
downside protection and risk-adjusted returns, focusing on contracted, inﬂation-linked cash ﬂows — closer to
Macquarie’s infrastructure approach than KKR’s PE-style value creation.

Hold periods: 5–10 years for value-add; Stonepeak’s infrastructure fund lifecycle is typically 10–12 years with
extensions.

Target returns: Mid-teens net IRR for ﬂagship funds, with strong emphasis on capital preservation and
downside protection. Stonepeak’s pitch to LPs is risk-adjusted returns — not the highest absolute returns, but the
most consistent and protected.

Key Takeaways for Conference Conversations

1. The Great Divergence is asset-type driven. Data centre multiples have exploded (28–35x+ in 2025)

while ﬁbre multiples have compressed (12–18x). The AI demand supercycle is repricing data centres as the
most valuable digital infrastructure asset class. Towers are stable but mature. This matters for every capital
strategy discussion at the conference — the PE panel, Cellnex, and the ﬁbre companies are living in
different valuation universes.

2. Macquarie’s TDC NET is the test case for the “infrastructure industrialist” model in telecom. Full
ownership, 72.8% EBITDA margin, aggressive copper decommissioning, sustainability-linked ﬁnancing.
If Macquarie achieves a 10–13x exit in a consolidated Danish market, it validates the thesis. If the market
stays fragmented and FCF stays negative, the thesis weakens.

3. KKR is the only investor playing at continental scale in European digital infrastructure. With

~$34bn in digital infra equity, KKR is building the connective tissue of European digital infrastructure —
from TIM’s Italian ﬁbre to Dutch ﬁbre to European data centres to global towers. No other single investor
has comparable breadth. This gives KKR structural advantages in deal origination, cross-portfolio
synergies, and regulatory relationships.

4. Pension funds (OMERS) vs. PE (KKR) vs. hybrid (Macquarie) represent three fundamentally
different capital structures with different time horizons, return targets, and risk tolerances.
Understanding which type of capital backs an asset tells you how that asset will be managed, how long it
will be held, and what the exit pathway looks like. This is essential context for every infrastructure
discussion at the conference.
