Executive Brieﬁng Note — NSR/BCG Conference
2026

State of the Industry

European telecom enters 2026 in a cautiously optimistic posture. After a decade of stagnant revenue, eroding
returns, and regulatory constraint, three structural shifts are converging: the regulatory climate is the most
permissive toward consolidation in a generation (Draghi Report, Vodafone-Three precedent), AI is ﬁnally
offering a credible path to 15–25% opex reduction, and ﬁbre/5G investment cycles are approaching their
inﬂection from cash-burn to cash-harvest in several major markets. The sector’s median EBITDA growth of
~2.5% is modest, but the variance between leaders and laggards is widening — operators with disciplined
pricing, enterprise digital capabilities, and advanced network infrastructure are pulling away.

The capital structure of European telecom has been fundamentally rewired. Over €80bn in infrastructure assets
have been carved out, spun off, or sold to PE and infrastructure funds since 2020. TIM’s NetCo sale, Cellnex’s
shift from acquirer to capital returner, and the UK altnet investment boom represent three phases of the same
trend: the unbundling of connectivity into specialized, separately-capitalized layers. Whether this disaggregation
creates lasting value or just redistributes risk is the central debate.

The unresolved tension is growth. European telecom revenue grows at ~2% while the companies it connects
grow at 8–15%. B2B digital services, platform diversiﬁcation (energy, ﬁntech), and network API monetization
are the most-discussed growth vectors — but collectively they still represent <15% of sector revenue. The
conference will test whether the industry’s growth narrative is supported by evidence or remains aspirational.

Three Overarching Narratives That Will Shape the Day

1. “Consolidation works — now do it again.” VodafoneThree is 10 months post-merger and will present
early evidence that 4-to-3 consolidation delivers investment, not just cost synergies. Every speaker and
attendee will be thinking about which European market is next. Marc Zedler’s DG-Comp session will be
parsed for any signal on Brussels’ appetite.

2. “Show me the money on AI.” Every operator has an AI strategy; few have measurable ROI. The

conference will push for speciﬁcity: what has actually been deployed at scale, what are the cash savings,
and what is the roadmap from pilot to platform? BCG’s closing session will attempt to benchmark the
industry.

3. “The capital cycle is turning.” Cellnex returning capital ahead of plan, BT approaching its FCF

inﬂection, TIM restoring dividends — the narrative is shifting from “invest and leverage” to “harvest and
return.” PE investors are evaluating exits, operators are approaching capex peaks, and the market is
rewarding cash generation over growth spending.

Market Context: 2021–2026 Retrospective

The European mobile connectivity landscape has undergone structural transformation since 2021. The initial
promise of 5G — heralded as a €620bn GDP contribution opportunity by the GSMA — has collided with the
reality of slow SA rollout, consumer indifference to speed upgrades, and persistent pricing pressure from four-
player mobile markets.

By end-2025, 5G SA remained commercially available in fewer than half of EU member states, with most
deployments still running in Non-Standalone (NSA) mode piggybacking on 4G cores (GSMA, 2025). This
contrasts sharply with South Korea, where 5G SA reached 50%+ population coverage by 2024, and the US,
where T-Mobile’s SA network covers 300m+ people. The investment gap is real: European mobile capex-to-
revenue ratios have trended around 15–17%, compared to 20%+ in leading Asian markets (ETNO, 2025).

Meanwhile, the convergence narrative has accelerated. Fixed-mobile convergence, once a niche strategy pursued
by operators like Orange and KPN, has become the central competitive framework. The logic is simple:
converged customers churn 30–40% less and generate 15–25% higher ARPU than single-play customers (BCG,
2024). This economic logic drove both the Vodafone-Three UK merger and Proximus’s ﬁbre-mobile bundling
strategy in Belgium.

The emergence of non-terrestrial networks adds another dimension. Starlink’s Direct-to-Cell technology, AST
SpaceMobile’s commercial trials, and operator partnerships (notably VMO2-Starlink and T-Mobile US–SpaceX)
are creating a viable path to ubiquitous coverage that bypasses traditional rural network economics.

Structural Dynamics: What Is Driving Change

Technology push: 5G SA enables network slicing, low-latency enterprise applications, and edge computing —
none of which work on NSA deployments. The industry is ﬁnally reaching the technical threshold where 5G-
speciﬁc use cases become deliverable. AI-RAN integration (e.g., Nokia-Nvidia’s $1bn partnership) is
accelerating network intelligence, promising 20–30% improvements in spectral efﬁciency and energy
optimization (Telecoms.com, 2026).

Capital requirements: The dual investment cycle — simultaneous ﬁbre and 5G SA builds — is straining
operator balance sheets. BT’s capex-to-sales ratio remains ~24% as it builds to 25m ﬁbre premises while
upgrading mobile (BT, FY25). VodafoneThree has committed to £1.3bn capex in its ﬁrst year alone (Vodafone,
2025). This capital intensity is pushing operators toward network sharing, infrastructure spin-offs, and
partnership models.

Regulatory enablement: The Draghi Report’s call for consolidation and investment-friendly regulation is
beginning to shift the policy landscape. DG-Comp’s approval of the Vodafone-Three merger (with conditions)
set a precedent for future 4-to-3 deals. Ofcom’s approach to ﬁbre regulation — allowing pricing ﬂexibility for
Openreach while maintaining access obligations — provides a model for balancing investment and competition.

Demand evolution: Consumer usage is migrating to video-heavy, always-on patterns — but increasingly on Wi-
Fi, not mobile networks. The counterargument that “most smartphone data runs on Wi-Fi” undermines some of
the case for massive mid-band spectrum allocations. Enterprise demand, particularly for private 5G networks
(800+ deployed or planned across Europe by end 2023, per ABI Research), represents the more defensible
growth vector.
