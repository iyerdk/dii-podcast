Top 10 Hallway Topics — NSR/BCG Conference
2026

1. “Which market is next for mobile consolidation?”

The Vodafone-Three precedent has opened the ﬂoodgates for speculation. Goldman Sachs identiﬁes Italy,
Sweden, Denmark, and Germany as the most likely next markets. The Draghi Report provides political cover;
DG-Comp’s evolving stance provides regulatory runway. But ﬁnancing, political will, and competitive dynamics
vary enormously by market. Every PE investor and operator CEO in the room has a view.

Opener: “With Vodafone-Three done, I’m curious — where do you think the next deal happens, and who makes
the ﬁrst move?”

2. “Is the UK altnet market heading for a shakeout?”

CityFibre at 4.7m premises and growing, VMO2 acquiring Netomnia, and dozens of sub-scale builders
struggling to reach proﬁtability. Analysts predict the UK altnet sector is entering “its most dangerous phase.” The
question is how many altnets survive as independents vs. being absorbed, and whether the shakeout reprices the
whole sector.

Opener: “I saw the ISPreview analysis calling this the altnets’ most dangerous phase. Do you agree — and who
are the survivors?”

3. “AI in telecom: where’s the actual ROI?”

Every operator has an AI strategy; BCG says 15–25% opex reduction is possible. But the gap between pilot and
production remains wide. The conference will push for speciﬁcity — what has been deployed at scale, what are
the cash savings, and what’s the realistic timeline for AI to materially impact margins?

Opener: “Everyone talks about AI saving 15–25% of opex. In your experience, what’s the real number so far —
and where has it actually worked?”

4. “BT’s free cash ﬂow inﬂection — real or aspirational?”

BT’s £2bn FCF FY27 target is the most closely watched milestone in European telecom. If achieved, it validates
the thesis that infrastructure investment can generate real returns. If missed, it validates the bears who say UK
ﬁbre competition makes returns structurally challenging. Kirkby’s session will be parsed for conﬁdence signals.

Opener: “Do you believe BT will hit £2bn FCF in FY27? What could go wrong?”

5. “Liberty Global’s discount — how does it close?”

more transactions. The debate: is this a genuine value opportunity or a perennial value trap? And is a take-private

Opener: “Liberty’s been trading at a discount for years. What’s it going to take to ﬁnally close the gap —
another spin-off, a take-private, or something else?”

6. “What is TIM’s identity post-NetCo?”

Labriola has transformed TIM from a debt-laden integrated operator to an asset-light ServCo. But the question
remains: can a telco without a ﬁxed network sustain competitive differentiation? The energy/utility platform play
is bold but unproven. TIM’s success or failure will inﬂuence every operator considering infrastructure separation.

Opener: “Pietro’s selling energy to SMEs now. Is TIM still a telco — or is it becoming something else
entirely?”

7. “Cellnex: done buying, but what’s the next growth engine?”

Cellnex has shifted from Europe’s most aggressive tower acquirer to a disciplined capital returner. The market
loves it — but the question is whether organic growth (4–5% revenue CAGR from co-location and new services)
can sustain the valuation without acquisitions. The tower business model’s long-term resilience in a
consolidating operator market is also under debate.

Opener: “Cellnex is returning capital ahead of plan, which is great. But with the acquisition engine off, where
does growth come from — and is 4–5% organic enough?”

8. “6 GHz spectrum: mobile or Wi-Fi?”

This is the spectrum policy debate of the decade. Mobile operators want it for 5G-Advanced/6G; the tech
industry wants it for Wi-Fi 7/8. The resolution will be political, determined by WRC-27 and national
implementation. European regulators are tentatively favoring mobile, but the Wi-Fi camp has strong arguments
about indoor coverage and consumer usage patterns.

Opener: “Where do you come out on 6 GHz — mobile or Wi-Fi? And is the industry over-stating spectrum
scarcity to build a case for 6G?”

9. “The Draghi effect — real change or just talk?”

Mario Draghi’s competitiveness report called for telecom consolidation, deregulation, and investment incentives.
A year and a half later, the question is whether it has produced tangible policy change or remains a political
document with limited operational impact. DG-Comp’s framework hasn’t fundamentally changed; national
regulators move at their own pace.

Opener: “Is the Draghi Report actually changing anything in practice, or is it the telecom equivalent of thoughts
and prayers?”

10. “Nordic consolidation — is it ﬁnally going to happen?”

Goldman Sachs has identiﬁed Sweden and Denmark as among the most likely next consolidation markets. The
Nordic regulatory environment may be more conducive than the adversarial UK process. But the 2015
TeliaSonera-Telenor Denmark failure casts a long shadow. With the Draghi-era political support and the
VodafoneThree precedent, the conditions may ﬁnally be right — but execution remains complex.

Opener: “As a Nordic operator, I’m watching the consolidation debate closely. Do you think Sweden or
Denmark moves ﬁrst — and what would trigger it?”
