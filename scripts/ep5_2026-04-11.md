## Segment 1 — CoreWeave's 48-Hour Coronation

**MAYA:** Let us start with the CoreWeave story, because it deserves a full accounting. On April ninth, CoreWeave disclosed an expanded agreement with Meta to provide AI cloud capacity through December 2032 — valued at twenty-one billion dollars. That number builds on a fourteen-point-two billion dollar agreement signed just six months earlier, in September 2025. The combined Meta commitment now stands at thirty-five billion dollars. To put that in context: CoreWeave went public with a contracted backlog of sixty-six-point-eight billion dollars. Meta alone now accounts for more than half of that figure.

**JAMES:** The technical specification of the deal matters. CoreWeave will deploy capacity across multiple data centres incorporating some of the first commercial-scale deployments of Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform — the GPU architecture Nvidia has positioned as its next-generation AI training system. For Meta, which is managing the largest privately funded AI infrastructure programme in history at a hundred to a hundred and thirty-five billion dollars in capital expenditure this year, securing early access to Vera Rubin through CoreWeave is both a capacity and a technology positioning move. At that scale, having a committed, GPU-rich cloud provider as a supplemental infrastructure layer is not a convenience. It is an operational necessity.

**MAYA:** The Anthropic announcement on April tenth was structurally different but strategically complementary. The financial terms were not disclosed, but the signal was precise: CoreWeave is not only the overflow cloud for hyperscalers managing peak demand. It is now the primary compute infrastructure for Anthropic — a frontier AI lab building models that require sustained, high-density GPU access at a scale that exceeds what any single commercial cloud can efficiently provide. With nine of the world's ten leading AI model providers now on CoreWeave's platform, the company has achieved something remarkable in the space of its first year as a public company: it has become a critical dependency for the entire AI model tier.

**JAMES:** The financial structure beneath that achievement deserves scrutiny. Simultaneously with the Anthropic announcement, CoreWeave disclosed it is raising three-point-five billion dollars through a convertible debt offering. The company is pre-profit. Its contracted backlog exceeds sixty-six billion dollars, it holds thirty-five billion in Meta commitments alone — and it is still burning capital faster than it generates it. That is not an indictment of the business model. GPU infrastructure requires enormous upfront capital commitment against contracts that deliver revenue over years, not quarters. But it is a reminder that the dependency running through CoreWeave is bilateral: the AI labs depend on CoreWeave for compute, and CoreWeave depends on the debt markets to keep building. The convertible offering is the other side of every deal headline.

**MAYA:** For investors watching this sector, the CoreWeave week crystallises a thesis we have been building since Episode One. The neocloud category — companies that exist specifically to provide GPU-dense compute to AI workloads — has moved from an experimental category to a critical infrastructure layer in under three years. CoreWeave's trajectory from startup to thirty-five billion dollar counterparty in twelve months as a public company is not an anomaly. It is the market acknowledging that there is an infrastructure layer between Nvidia's chips and Meta's AI products — and that layer requires specialised capital and operational expertise that neither hyperscalers nor traditional data centre operators can fully internalise.


## Segment 2 — OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK: Energy Is the Deal

**MAYA:** On the same day as the CoreWeave-Meta announcement, Bloomberg reported that OpenAI had paused its Stargate UK project — an AI data centre initiative announced in September 2025 with Nvidia and Nscale, intended to deploy eight thousand GPUs across north-east England, scalable to thirty-one thousand. The reasons OpenAI gave: high energy costs and regulatory uncertainty. In that order.

**JAMES:** The energy cost number is not abstract. UK industrial electricity prices sit at approximately twenty-four-point-seven pence per kilowatt hour as of April 2026 — still thirty-five percent above pre-energy-crisis levels even after the modest reduction in the Q2 price cap. Independent analysis consistently finds that UK industrial power costs run at approximately four times the rate of comparable US industrial users. For a data centre deployment — where power is the single largest operating expense after capital amortisation — that differential is not a friction. It is a fundamental investment case problem.

**MAYA:** The UK government has been aware of this problem. It has promised a "targeted pricing support mechanism" for AI Growth Zone data centres, describing discounts commensurate with constraint cost savings. But that mechanism has not been finalised or operationalised. OpenAI's pause is the direct cost of that implementation gap: a company publicly committed to significant UK investment has suspended its deployment timeline because the government support that would have made the economics viable does not yet exist in functional form.

**JAMES:** The regulatory dimension is equally relevant, and somewhat underreported. OpenAI cited "ongoing regulatory developments affecting AI models" — a reference to the UK's unsettled position on AI training and copyright law. The company has been engaged in active negotiations with the UK government about the legal framework for training data use. Without that framework resolved, investing in UK-based training infrastructure carries legal exposure that complicates long-duration capital deployment. Both factors — energy and regulation — are problems that have been visible for some time. The Stargate UK pause is the point at which their combined weight became decisive.

**MAYA:** The broader read-through is the one we have been building since Episode Three. Markets that attract AI infrastructure investment in 2026 are those that resolve the binding constraints: power cost, grid availability, and regulatory predictability. The UK meets none of those three cleanly right now. Contrast that with the Nordic markets, where power costs are structurally lower, grid connections are available without multi-year queues, and the regulatory environment for data processing is stable and predictable. When OpenAI signals it needs "the right conditions to enable long-term infrastructure investment," it is describing a checklist that the Nordics already satisfy, and that the UK is still assembling. That gap is now measured in paused GPU deployments — and the investment signal those pauses send to every other operator evaluating UK commitments is not a subtle one.


## Segment 3 — Tariff Phase Two: The April 14 Decision Point

**MAYA:** On Tuesday, April fourteenth — three days from now — the United States Secretary of Commerce and the US Trade Representative must deliver to the President their ninety-day report on the outcome of Phase One semiconductor tariff negotiations. This deadline was written into Trump's January fourteenth Section 232 executive order, and it is the formal trigger for determining whether the tariff programme advances to Phase Two.

**JAMES:** Phase Two, as the executive order describes it, would broaden the tariff scope to a wider range of semiconductor derivative products — assembled servers, networking hardware, and power systems that the current Phase One carve-out does not cover, but which every data centre build requires in bulk. Phase Two would come with a tariff rate described as "significant" and, critically, a new tariff offset programme: companies investing in US domestic chip production would receive preferential treatment, turning the tariff into a leverage mechanism for reshoring manufacturing.

**MAYA:** In Episode Four, we documented how the Liberation Day tariff structure had already exposed a critical gap — the bare chip exemption protects the most expensive component in an AI server rack, but leaves everything surrounding it exposed to levies running from ten to forty-five percent. Phase Two, if activated, would formalise and potentially expand that exposure. The April fourteenth report is unlikely to produce immediate visible action — these reports are typically followed by a decision period before Presidential action. But the market will be watching closely for any indication of the administration's Phase Two appetite, and for signals about the July first data centre chip review, which remains the most consequential single regulatory event for US AI infrastructure economics in 2026.

**JAMES:** The downstream implications are already visible in procurement behaviour. Data centre operators who have been frontloading hardware purchases since Liberation Day — compressing months of planned procurement into weeks — have extended lead times further in the process. The hardware crunch that was running at twenty-four to fifty-two weeks for AI components has stretched. If the April fourteenth report signals imminent Phase Two broadening, we should expect another acceleration round — and with it, further strain on supply chains that are operating at capacity and with no significant slack. The tariff timeline and the hardware supply timeline are converging.


## Segment 4 — Aligned Data Centers: The $40 Billion Standard

**MAYA:** The anticipated close — expected before the end of the first half of 2026 — of the $40 billion sale of Aligned Data Centers will set the high-water mark for AI infrastructure valuations for the foreseeable future. Macquarie Asset Management is selling the company to a consortium comprising the AI Infrastructure Partnership — which includes BlackRock's Global Infrastructure Partners, Microsoft, and Nvidia — alongside Abu Dhabi's MGX, with Kuwait Investment Authority and Singapore's Temasek as anchor investors.

**JAMES:** The Macquarie exit story is the one that every infrastructure PE investor in this sector will study. When Macquarie acquired Aligned, the company operated two data centres in Dallas and Phoenix with eighty-five megawatts of combined capacity. At exit, the portfolio has grown to five gigawatts of operational and planned capacity across fifty facilities in the US, Mexico, Brazil, Chile, and Colombia — approximately a sixty-fold expansion. The forty-billion-dollar valuation reflects the multiple that kind of operational performance generates when an asset is perfectly positioned for the moment of AI infrastructure demand acceleration. This is the Macquarie "infrastructure industrialist" model — buy, operate, optimise, exit — producing a result that validates the thesis at scale.

**MAYA:** The buyer consortium is structurally significant beyond its price tag. The AI Infrastructure Partnership was co-founded specifically to deploy institutional capital into AI infrastructure at scale, with a stated target of thirty billion dollars initially and up to one hundred billion including debt. Aligned is AIP's first investment. The participation of Microsoft and Nvidia as founding AIP members — alongside sovereign capital from Abu Dhabi, Kuwait, and Singapore — represents a new level of institutionalisation for AI infrastructure financing. When national sovereign wealth funds and the companies that manufacture the chips and operate the platforms converge on a single infrastructure asset, it is not speculative capital. It is strategic positioning by three continents simultaneously.

**JAMES:** The valuation implied by the forty-billion-dollar transaction is the data point that resets benchmarks across the entire sector. The previous record was Blackstone's acquisition of AirTrunk in 2024 at sixteen-point-six billion dollars — itself a record at the time, and the deal we referenced in our context on Macquarie's track record. Aligned has reset that ceiling by approximately a hundred and forty percent in under two years. For every infrastructure fund holding data centre assets, for every operator considering a sale process, and for every analyst modelling AI colocation values, the Aligned transaction has just fundamentally repriced the asset class.


## Segment 5 — WorldLink: Routing Around the Gulf

**MAYA:** We close this week with a story that connects directly to Episode One. In March, we described how the Gulf conflict had exposed the critical vulnerability of seventeen submarine cables running through the Red Sea corridor — carrying the majority of internet traffic between Europe, Africa, and Asia. This week, a UAE-Iraqi consortium announced it is moving from diagnosis to structural response. The WorldLink project is a seven-hundred-million-dollar hybrid submarine and terrestrial cable system connecting the UAE to Turkey via Iraq, with branches to five Gulf Cooperation Council states: the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait.

**JAMES:** The technical specification is precise and deliberate. WorldLink is designed to carry nine hundred terabits per second of capacity — comparable to the largest currently operating subsea cables — while maintaining latency between Europe and the Middle East consistently below one hundred milliseconds. That latency ceiling is the critical enterprise specification: real-time AI inference, high-frequency financial trading, and distributed enterprise applications have hard latency requirements that cannot be met through rerouting via African or Indian Ocean alternatives during a disruption scenario. WorldLink is engineered specifically to meet those requirements on a route that does not cross the Red Sea at any point.

**MAYA:** The consortium — Iraqi firms Tech 964 and DIL Technology, and Emirati-based Breeze Investments — represents regional capital solving a regional connectivity problem. The project is privately funded, with construction planned in phases over five years. The strategic logic is straightforward: the Gulf conflict has demonstrated with operational specificity that the internet routing architecture built on the assumption of stable Red Sea access cannot be relied upon. WorldLink creates the first purpose-built alternative at a scale that provides genuine redundancy rather than marginal backup capacity.

**JAMES:** There is a tension embedded in this project that investors should hold clearly. WorldLink is the infrastructure response to regional instability — and it is building through a region that remains affected by that same instability. Some reports have flagged questions about whether the construction timeline can be maintained given the geographic context. The permanent rerouting project is itself operating in the threat environment it is designed to solve. That is the defining challenge of critical connectivity infrastructure investment in contested regions: the most valuable redundancy assets are also the most difficult to build. The same risk geography that creates the demand for WorldLink is the risk that the project itself must navigate.

**MAYA:** For investors in subsea and terrestrial connectivity, WorldLink is a data point about where the market is heading. Hyperscalers have driven the majority of new cable investment for a decade. WorldLink is different: it is regionally motivated, privately funded, and driven by geopolitical resilience rather than AI bandwidth demand. As the conflict geography of the 2020s hardens, we should expect more of these regionally motivated connectivity investments — and their sizing and routing will follow the threat map, not the traffic map.


## What to Watch — Four Decisions Ahead

**JAMES:** Four developments to monitor in the days and weeks ahead. First: the April fourteenth Commerce Department and USTR report on Phase One semiconductor tariff negotiations. The scope and tone of that report will signal whether Phase Two broadening is imminent, delayed, or being negotiated away through bilateral trading partner deals. Any indication that the data centre chip carve-out is under active review before July first will move hardware procurement timelines immediately.

**MAYA:** Second: CoreWeave's Q1 2026 earnings, expected in late April. This will be the company's first earnings call with thirty-five billion dollars in Meta commitments and the Anthropic deal on the books. The market will be looking for revenue recognition rates against those commitments, capital expenditure guidance relative to the three-point-five billion convertible raise, and any Vera Rubin supply update. It will also be the first formal test of whether the neocloud valuation survives contact with the financial reality of running GPU infrastructure at this scale.

**JAMES:** Third: the UK government's timeline for operationalising its AI Growth Zone energy discount. OpenAI's Stargate UK pause has placed the government under direct pressure to accelerate. The question is whether the "targeted pricing support" commitment translates into a concrete mechanism — and whether that mechanism arrives quickly enough to reverse the investment signal OpenAI has sent. A credible implementation plan could reactivate paused projects. A continued gap between policy commitment and operational reality will attract further withdrawals from other operators currently evaluating UK deployments.

**MAYA:** And fourth: the first Q1 2026 hyperscaler earnings calls, beginning in late April. These will be the first formal capex guidance updates since Liberation Day, and the first opportunity for CEOs to quantify tariff exposure on official guidance rather than analyst estimates. The gap between the six-hundred-plus billion dollars of projected aggregate hyperscaler capex and what the earnings calls actually confirm will be the most closely watched data point in the infrastructure sector this quarter. Every data centre operator, every construction supplier, and every GPU vendor is building their second-half planning around what those numbers say.
