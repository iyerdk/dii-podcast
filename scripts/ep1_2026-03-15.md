## Geopolitics — Iran, Hormuz, and the Seventeen Cables

**MAYA:** Let's start with the story that will define infrastructure investment strategy for the next several years — whether or not the conflict itself escalates further. On March fourteenth, Alcatel Submarine Networks declared what is called force majeure on the construction of Meta's 2Africa Pearls cable extension. Force majeure is a legal term meaning: circumstances beyond our control have made it impossible to proceed. Their installation vessel — the Île de Batz — is stranded in Dammam, Saudi Arabia. The work linking Gulf states, Pakistan, and India to a broader global network has stopped.

[NOTE: brief pause here — let the physical reality land]

**JAMES:** And this matters well beyond the immediate disruption, because it illustrates a structural vulnerability that the industry has known about for decades but consistently underinvested in addressing. Seventeen submarine cables run through the Red Sea corridor. These are not backup systems. They carry the majority of internet traffic between Europe, Africa, and Asia. There are fewer than one hundred cable repair vessels in the entire world — and during active military hostilities, none of them can enter the conflict zone. The global investment gap in cable repair capacity alone is estimated at three billion dollars. That is not a large number relative to what hyperscalers are spending on data centres this year — and yet it has never been filled.

[PULLQUOTE: "Route diversification is no longer a planning preference — it is a fiduciary obligation."]

**MAYA:** That quote, which emerged from industry discussions this week, is worth sitting with. The board-level framing matters: this is not an operational question for network engineers. It is a governance question for directors and investors.

**JAMES:** Right, and one company that anticipated this, deliberately, is Meta. Its Project Waterworth — the longest planned undersea cable in history at fifty thousand kilometres — was specifically routed to avoid the Red Sea corridor. That routing decision, taken years ago, is now worth billions in avoided disruption. It is one of the clearest examples this cycle of long-duration infrastructure thinking creating measurable competitive advantage.

**MAYA:** And the macro read-through for European and Nordic investors is significant. If Hormuz remains closed for an extended period, oil-driven inflation keeps central banks cautious. The US Federal Reserve's rate-cut timeline moves further out. Long-duration infrastructure assets — data centres, fibre networks — face higher financing costs. This is not hypothetical: the ten-year Treasury yield moved from three-point-nine-seven percent to four-point-two-eight percent in two weeks. That is a meaningful repricing.


## Data Centers — $700 Billion and the Infrastructure Deficit

**MAYA:** Now to the number that stopped people mid-sentence in boardrooms this week. Combined forward data centre lease obligations for Microsoft, Meta, Google, and Amazon have exceeded seven hundred billion dollars. That figure, published by Bloomberg on March twelfth, represents legally binding commitments — rent, in effect, for future capacity.

**JAMES:** And the individual numbers are staggering. Microsoft alone holds a hundred and fifty-five billion in these obligations. Meta is at a hundred and four billion. Microsoft paused leasing through much of 2025, then returned to the market urgently — its executives acknowledged publicly that the company does not have sufficient data centre capacity to meet current demand.

[NOTE: This is the structural demand signal — frame it as such]

**MAYA:** The most vivid illustration of the scale here is CoreWeave. CoreWeave is what the industry calls an AI neocloud — a company that builds and operates GPU-dense data centres for AI workloads. It guided this week for twelve to thirteen billion dollars in revenue in 2026. That is up from five-point-one billion in 2025 — more than doubling in a single year.

**JAMES:** It holds a contracted backlog of sixty-six-point-eight billion dollars, including twenty-two billion from OpenAI and fourteen billion from Meta. And here's the number that really makes you stop — its 2026 capital expenditure guidance of thirty to thirty-five billion dollars would exceed the national infrastructure budgets of most European countries. Let that context register.

**MAYA:** So what should European and Nordic executives take from this? Two things. First, the demand is real and the commitments are contractual. This is not speculative capex chasing a trend — these are locked leases with named counterparties.

**JAMES:** And second — and this matters for Nordic markets specifically — the regulatory and community backlash in the United States is accelerating. More than three hundred data centre bills have been filed across thirty US states in the first six weeks of 2026. Ohio, Oklahoma, Arizona, and Michigan are moving toward moratoriums or zoning restrictions. Georgia repealed sales tax exemptions and is requiring operators to fund their own infrastructure costs.

**MAYA:** That US-level friction is a structural tailwind for markets that offer stable permitting, renewable energy, and cooling advantages. Denmark, Sweden, and Finland — already home to Nebius, Microsoft, Google, and Meta campuses — are direct beneficiaries of that displacement.


## Investment · Nordic Focus — Nvidia Bets $2 Billion on Amsterdam — and the Nordic Edge

**MAYA:** On March eleventh, Nvidia invested two billion dollars into Nebius Group, an Amsterdam-based AI infrastructure company. Nebius's stock rose eighteen percent on the announcement.

**JAMES:** This transaction is strategically interesting on multiple levels. Nvidia is not just a chip supplier to Nebius — it is now an equity investor in a company that buys Nvidia chips. It is, in effect, seeding its own demand. That is a new chapter in Nvidia's business model — moving from component supplier to ecosystem investor.

**MAYA:** But for our audience, the Nordic dimension is the most important part of this story. Nebius plans sixteen to twenty billion dollars in capital expenditure this year. It targets more than five gigawatts of deployed Nvidia systems by 2030. And critically — its primary infrastructure footprint is in Denmark, Sweden, and Finland.

**JAMES:** And the reasons are straightforward but powerful. Renewable energy at competitive rates. Natural cooling that reduces energy costs by thirty to forty percent compared to warmer markets. And political stability that infrastructure investors — particularly those with ten-to-twenty year return horizons — value more than almost any other variable. The Nordics are not emerging as a data centre hub. They have already emerged. This Nvidia transaction is validation at the highest level of the global technology industry.


## Fiber & Fixed Networks — Google Exits Fiber. Private Capital Takes the Network.

**MAYA:** One of this week's more structurally significant moves went somewhat under the radar given the geopolitical noise. Google — through its parent Alphabet — is exiting the consumer fibre business. Alphabet announced it is combining its GFiber business with Stonepeak's Astound Broadband, creating a national-scale independent broadband operator. Stonepeak takes majority ownership; Alphabet retains a minority stake. The combined entity is a direct challenger to Comcast and Charter.

**JAMES:** Why does this matter structurally? Because it confirms a pattern. The hyperscalers are not in the business of running consumer-facing fixed networks. That is a different operating model — capital-intensive, customer-service heavy, regulatory-sensitive. The hyperscalers want to buy capacity from networks, not own them at the consumer layer.

**MAYA:** Exactly. And infrastructure private equity — Stonepeak, Macquarie, EQT, KKR — is moving into the gap. Total US fibre asset-backed securities issuance now exceeds fourteen billion dollars. Surf Internet alone closed a four-hundred-and-seven million dollar securitisation this week, with Goldman Sachs.

**JAMES:** In the enterprise segment, Lumen's AI-driven fibre contracts are approaching thirteen billion dollars, following its selection to expand Anthropic's network across North America. For European investors, the parallel question is: does this pattern repeat on your side of the Atlantic? The answer, based on current M&A trajectories, appears to be yes — just on a slightly delayed timeline.


## Towers & Wireless — A $476 Million Default and a €50 Billion European Consolidation

**MAYA:** The tower sector has two distinct stories this week — one cautionary, one strategic. Let's take the cautionary one first. The EchoStar-Dish tower lease default has now been quantified. Combined annual revenue exposure across American Tower, Crown Castle, and SBA Communications is four hundred and seventy-six million dollars.

**JAMES:** Barclays downgraded both American Tower and Crown Castle this week. American Tower's 2026 organic growth guidance is now approximately one percent — versus the four percent the market had expected. That is a significant miss, and it reflects the real credit risk embedded in tower REIT tenant concentrations.

**MAYA:** The strategic story is in Europe, and it is moving at mega-deal scale. Ardian and Brookfield are reportedly working a joint bid for INWIT — Italy's leading tower company — at an implied eight to eleven billion euros.

**JAMES:** And more significantly, American Tower is in reported discussions regarding Cellnex at a potential fifty billion euro valuation. If that transaction proceeds, it would be one of the largest infrastructure acquisitions in European history.

**MAYA:** On the construction side — and this is a longer-term signal — T-Mobile's CEO proposed building six thousand new rural macro towers using federal BEAD funding over five years in the United States. That would represent a decade-high construction cycle in US wireless infrastructure.


## Power & Energy — Power Generation Is Now Digital Infrastructure

**MAYA:** If there is one structural shift this week that investors should internalise above all others, it is this: power generation and digital infrastructure are no longer separate asset classes. They are converging — and the transactions this week confirm it conclusively.

**JAMES:** The numbers bear that out completely. EQT and BlackRock's Global Infrastructure Partners agreed to acquire AES Corporation — the power utility supplying Google, Microsoft, and Amazon data centres — for ten-point-seven billion dollars in equity and thirty-three-point-four billion in enterprise value. In the same week, KKR announced a fifty billion dollar partnership with ECP focused on power generation for digital infrastructure. And Google completed its four-point-seven-five billion dollar acquisition of Intersect Power.

**MAYA:** These are not coincidental. They reflect a calculation that the companies most likely to face power constraints are the ones best positioned to own the generation assets themselves — removing both cost uncertainty and supply risk from their balance sheets.

**JAMES:** For European and Nordic investors, the parallel is direct. The Nordics' hydroelectric base, Scandinavia's offshore wind pipeline, and Northern Europe's interconnection infrastructure are not just energy assets. They are, increasingly, digital infrastructure assets. The convergence thesis is not coming — it has arrived.


## Investor Roundup — Who Moved, and How Much

**MAYA:** A brief investor landscape summary, because the capital flows this week were significant in breadth as well as scale. KKR has committed thirty-four billion dollars in equity across digital infrastructure, including the STT GDC acquisition at thirteen-point-eight billion enterprise value and its fifty billion power partnership with ECP.

**JAMES:** Macquarie acquired IHS Latin America towers for nine-hundred-and-fifty-two million, picked up Gabia's South Korean data centres for approximately four-hundred-and-twenty million, and is closing its MIP VI infrastructure fund above eight billion.

**MAYA:** GIC — Singapore's sovereign wealth fund — is co-invested in the Equinix xScale joint venture above fifteen billion and participated in Anthropic's Series G funding round. CPPIB — the Canada Pension Plan — is committed to atNorth and Equinix Nordic data centres at four billion, and is in a joint venture with Goodman for European data centre development at eight billion euros.

**JAMES:** What this breadth tells us is important: this is not a single-country or single-strategy wave. It is institutional capital from North America, Europe, and Asia converging simultaneously on the same thesis — with the Nordics and Northern Europe as a preferred landing zone.


## What to Watch — The Five Decisions That Follow This Week

**MAYA:** Five things to track in the days ahead. First: the FOMC meeting on March seventeenth and eighteenth. The dot plot update will signal the Federal Reserve's rate path for the second half of 2026. Given the oil-driven inflation pressure from the Hormuz closure, this is more consequential than a typical meeting. Watch for any shift in the median projection.

**JAMES:** Second: the Joliet, Illinois data centre vote on March sixteenth. This is a proposed twenty-billion-dollar campus. The outcome serves as a bellwether for whether US regulatory resistance is hardening or moderating.

**MAYA:** Third: the Hormuz situation, day by day. Duration is everything. Weeks of disruption is manageable. Months changes the investment environment materially.

**JAMES:** Fourth: Crown Castle's pending sale of its fibre and small cell business to Zayo and EQT. Expected to close in the first half of 2026 at eight-point-five billion. A significant test of PE appetite for fibre in the current rate environment.

**MAYA:** And fifth: Powell succession. Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell's term expires in May 2026. Kevin Warsh is reported as the leading candidate for the role. Warsh is considered hawkish — his appointment would likely signal a tighter rate stance, with direct implications for long-duration infrastructure financing.
