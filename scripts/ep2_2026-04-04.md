## Segment 1 — CoreWeave's $8.5 Billion Signal

**MAYA:** Let's start where the week started — with CoreWeave and what this loan actually means. CoreWeave is what the industry calls an AI neocloud. It does not sell cloud services in the traditional sense — it does not offer databases, object storage, or developer tools. It sells GPU compute, specifically for AI training and inference workloads, to AI labs and large enterprises that need dedicated capacity outside the hyperscaler ecosystem.

**JAMES:** And its growth trajectory has been almost disorienting to observe. Full-year 2025 revenue came in at five-point-one billion dollars — up one hundred and sixty-eight percent year-on-year. The company is guiding for over ten billion dollars in 2026. Its contracted backlog stands at sixty-six-point-eight billion, three hundred percent larger than a year ago. OpenAI and Meta together account for the majority of that backlog.

**MAYA:** The eight-point-five billion dollar facility — officially called a Delayed Draw Term Loan, or DDTL — was structured by MUFG and Morgan Stanley, with Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan as coordinating lead arrangers. It was anchored by Blackstone Credit and Insurance, and it was materially oversubscribed. It matures in March 2032 and is secured against the GPU clusters and infrastructure assets of a CoreWeave subsidiary.

**JAMES:** Now, the investment-grade rating is the real story here, and it has two dimensions. The first is what it says about CoreWeave's business model: the company has now demonstrated that GPU-backed infrastructure, when underpinned by long-duration contracts with named investment-grade counterparties, can support institutional credit structures. That legitimises the asset class for a much wider pool of lenders — pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign funds — who have strict rating mandates.

**MAYA:** The second dimension is the concentration risk question, which analysts are watching carefully. Nineteen-point-two billion dollars of that contract backlog is with Meta. If Meta were to reduce its CoreWeave commitment — even marginally — the impact on the company's financial profile would be significant. That is not a hypothesis; it is the primary risk factor in CoreWeave's own credit documentation. So we have an investment-grade rating resting, substantially, on a single counterparty. The market is pricing it as a strength today. Whether it remains so in twelve months is a genuine question.

**JAMES:** And this lands against the broader backdrop of hyperscaler capex acceleration that we tracked in our first episode. The four major hyperscalers — Amazon, Google, Meta, and Microsoft — are collectively on track to spend approximately seven hundred billion dollars in capital expenditure in 2026, up from roughly three hundred and eighty-four billion in 2025. Amazon alone is projected at two hundred billion dollars — a number that analysts note will push the company's free cash flow negative for the year. The capex arms race is not decelerating. It is accelerating. And CoreWeave's ability to raise institutional debt at investment grade is, in effect, the financial system catching up to the pace of the underlying demand.


## Segment 2 — NVIDIA's Vera Rubin and the Compute Supply Crunch

**MAYA:** From the financing story to the hardware story — because the two are inseparable. At NVIDIA's GTC conference in mid-March, Jensen Huang unveiled Vera Rubin — the company's next-generation GPU architecture, and the successor to the Blackwell platform that has been driving the current data centre buildout.

**JAMES:** The numbers are striking. Vera Rubin's NVL72 configuration — seventy-two Rubin GPUs paired with thirty-six Vera CPUs through NVIDIA's NVLink 6 interconnect — delivers three-point-six exaFLOPS of NVFP4 inference throughput. NVIDIA is claiming up to ten times the inference throughput per watt compared to Blackwell. For data centre operators, that ratio — performance per watt — is arguably more important than raw compute, because power is now the binding constraint in almost every market.

**MAYA:** But the more immediate story out of GTC is supply, not specifications. Jensen Huang confirmed at the conference that HBM4 memory — the next-generation high-bandwidth memory that Rubin depends on — is completely sold out through 2026. SK Hynix stated publicly that their entire 2026 HBM supply is committed. Samsung and SK Hynix have raised HBM3E prices approximately twenty percent for 2026 contracts. And TSMC's advanced packaging capacity — which is required to assemble these chip configurations — is also fully booked through year-end.

**JAMES:** For investors, this creates a two-speed dynamic. Companies that locked in supply agreements early — Microsoft, Meta, Google, Amazon — have a structural advantage over any competitor trying to enter or expand in AI infrastructure today. GPU lead times are now running between thirty-six and fifty-two weeks. That is not a chip shortage in the consumer electronics sense — it is a constraint on the pace of AI infrastructure deployment globally.

**MAYA:** And there is a parallel story on the economics side that is equally significant. Inference — the running of AI models once trained — now accounts for fifty-five percent of all AI infrastructure spending, up from thirty-three percent in 2023. That shift has two causes. First, AI applications are moving from labs into production at scale. Second, inference costs have collapsed dramatically — GPT-4-equivalent inference now costs approximately forty cents per million tokens, versus roughly twenty dollars in late 2022. A thousand-fold reduction in three years.

**JAMES:** The cost collapse is important context for the infrastructure investment argument. As inference costs fall, the addressable market for AI applications expands — which means the demand for compute infrastructure expands with it. Lower cost per token does not mean less compute demand. It means more use cases become economically viable, which drives more demand. The infrastructure investment cycle is not self-limiting. It is self-reinforcing.


## Segment 3 — Nuclear Goes Mainstream: From Pilot to Portfolio

**MAYA:** In Episode One, we traced the convergence of power generation and digital infrastructure — the thesis that energy assets are, increasingly, digital infrastructure assets. This week, that convergence moved into a new phase. Nuclear power for data centres is no longer a pilot programme or a speculative commitment. It is becoming a portfolio strategy.

**JAMES:** Let's put the scale on the table. Meta has signed twenty-year power purchase agreements with three nuclear operators — Oklo, Vistra, and TerraPower — covering up to six-point-six gigawatts of capacity by 2035. The Vistra agreements alone extend the life of two existing plants in Ohio totalling more than two-point-one gigawatts and uprate their combined capacity by four hundred and thirty-three megawatts. Pennsylvania now hosts three major nuclear-for-AI projects in various stages of development.

**MAYA:** Microsoft's agreement with Constellation Energy to restart Three Mile Island — the eight-hundred-and-thirty-seven megawatt facility in Pennsylvania — is advancing on schedule, with an expected return to service in 2028. Constellation has committed one-point-six billion dollars to the restart, backed by Microsoft's twenty-year power purchase agreement. The significance of Three Mile Island specifically is symbolic as well as practical: the facility became synonymous with nuclear risk after its 1979 partial meltdown. Its restart as a dedicated AI data centre power source is a reputational as well as a technical milestone for the nuclear industry.

**JAMES:** And the operator that made the most news this week is Equinix. In February, Equinix committed seven hundred million dollars to a new Hanley Energy facility in Ireland to accelerate power equipment production for its next-generation data centres. But the more significant development is its nuclear agreement portfolio, which now spans four different reactor technologies: Oklo's Aurora fission powerhouses at five hundred megawatts, twenty Kaleidos microreactors from Radiant, two hundred and fifty megawatts from ULC Energy via Rolls-Royce small modular reactors in the Netherlands, and five hundred megawatts from Stellaria's molten-salt fast-neutron SMR programme.

**MAYA:** That portfolio approach — spreading commitments across multiple reactor technologies and geographies — is deliberate. No single SMR developer has reached commercial deployment at scale. By anchoring multiple programmes simultaneously, Equinix is both hedging technology risk and, critically, acting as the kind of anchor customer that gives lenders and investors confidence to fund the reactor developers themselves. Equinix is not just buying power — it is financing the commercialisation of advanced nuclear.

**JAMES:** For the investor community, the read-through is this: power procurement is now a board-level strategic decision, not a facilities management function. The companies that secure long-duration, dispatchable power at predictable cost — whether through nuclear, hydro, or other firm sources — will have a structural operating advantage over those dependent on spot power markets. And the European dimension is directly relevant: the Nordics' hydroelectric base and Scandinavia's offshore wind pipeline are, in effect, pre-built versions of the strategic position these companies are now paying billions to construct from scratch.


## Segment 4 — Southeast Asia and the Middle East: The Next Buildout Wave

**MAYA:** Three weeks ago, we ended our first episode by noting that US regulatory friction — over three hundred data centre bills filed across thirty states in early 2026 — was creating a structural tailwind for markets with stable permitting, renewable energy, and political predictability. This week, the other side of that coin came into sharper focus. The markets absorbing that displaced investment momentum are no longer abstract destinations. They are specific, and the capital is moving.

**JAMES:** Starting in Southeast Asia, where the picture is becoming increasingly differentiated. Singapore closed the application window on its DC-CFA2 process — its second Data Centre Call for Application — on the thirty-first of March. That process is offering at least two hundred megawatts of new licensed data centre capacity in the city-state, with mandatory sustainability requirements. The significance is that Singapore is carefully rationing capacity rather than throwing open the doors — which has the effect of pushing adjacent demand into Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam.

**MAYA:** And Malaysia is now the most significant story in the region. It accounts for more than half of all under-construction data centre capacity across the five core Southeast Asian markets. Google has committed two billion dollars to develop its first Malaysian data centre and cloud region. Microsoft has earmarked two-point-two billion dollars for Malaysia. Johor, the Malaysian state bordering Singapore, has emerged as the primary landing zone — accessible to Singapore's cloud ecosystem but with land, power, and regulatory conditions that Singapore itself cannot offer at scale.

**JAMES:** A Fortune article published on March twenty-sixth noted one constraint that is worth flagging for long-term investors: heat management. Southeast Asia's ambient temperatures — regularly above thirty degrees Celsius — significantly increase the cost and complexity of data centre cooling compared to Northern European markets. The cooling efficiency gap between Singapore and, say, Helsinki, is estimated at thirty to forty percent of total energy cost. As AI compute densities increase, that gap widens. It is not a reason to discount Southeast Asia's growth trajectory — but it is a structural cost that the Nordics do not face.

**MAYA:** And then there is the Middle East, where the numbers are in a different category altogether. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund has announced the hundred-billion-dollar Transcendence AI Initiative — a programme to build AI infrastructure, data centres, and related capacity as part of Vision 2030's digital economy objectives. Amazon Web Services has committed five-point-three billion dollars to develop new data centres in the kingdom. The Middle East's total data centre capacity is projected to triple — from roughly one gigawatt today to three-point-three gigawatts within five years.

**JAMES:** For infrastructure investors, the Middle East story has a particular character. The capital is largely sovereign — PIF, ADIA, Mubadala — and the strategic motivation is economic diversification, not near-term return optimisation. That creates a different risk-return profile than commercial data centre investment. It is also creating genuine M&A and co-investment opportunities for global operators who can bring technical expertise and hyperscaler relationships that the sovereign funds cannot replicate internally.


## Segment 5 — Cables: The Infrastructure Beneath the Infrastructure

**MAYA:** Before we close, a subsea cable update — because we cannot discuss global digital infrastructure without revisiting the physical layer that connects all of it. Three weeks ago, we reported on the force majeure declaration by Alcatel Submarine Networks on Meta's 2Africa Pearls cable extension, with an installation vessel stranded in the conflict zone. The situation has not resolved.

**JAMES:** And the broader investment story has moved in parallel. Total investment in new subsea cable projects between 2025 and 2027 is running at approximately thirteen billion dollars — a multi-year record, driven almost entirely by hyperscaler-funded private cable initiatives. The strategic logic is straightforward: if the Red Sea corridor remains unreliable, redundant routes become commercially essential rather than merely prudent.

**MAYA:** This week, the Australian government announced a hundred-and-twenty-million-dollar programme to construct three new subsea cables linking Papua New Guinea, with Google selected to lead the construction. That is a small transaction by absolute standards — but it is illustrative of a broader pattern in which hyperscalers and governments are increasingly co-investing in cable infrastructure for both commercial and strategic reasons. Australia's interest in Papua New Guinea's connectivity is geopolitical as well as economic — a direct response to Chinese infrastructure investment in the Pacific island region.

**JAMES:** Malaysia's SALAM project — the Sambungan Kabel Dasar Laut MADANI — is also worth noting. It is a two-billion-ringgit investment, approximately four hundred and fifty million US dollars, to construct a thirty-one-hundred-kilometre subsea fibre cable linking Peninsula Malaysia to Sabah and Sarawak on the island of Borneo. That domestic cable project is infrastructure-as-sovereignty: reducing Malaysia's dependence on external routing for its own national connectivity, while simultaneously supporting the data centre and 5G backhaul demands of its rapidly expanding Johor market.

**MAYA:** The structural point we made in Episode One holds — and intensifies. Submarine cable infrastructure is no longer priced as a utility asset with stable, predictable returns. It is being repriced as strategic infrastructure with geopolitical optionality. The companies and funds that understand that distinction — and act on it ahead of the broader market — are the ones that will own the most valuable positions in the next decade of global connectivity investment.


## What to Watch — The Four Decisions Ahead

**JAMES:** Four things to watch in the days ahead. First: CoreWeave's stock reaction as the market fully digests the $8.5 billion loan and its Meta concentration implications. The investment-grade rating is a positive. The counterparty concentration is a risk. How institutional investors weight those two factors will tell us something important about AI infrastructure credit markets going forward.

**MAYA:** Second: Singapore's DC-CFA2 decisions. The applications closed March thirty-first. When Singapore announces its selections — and the associated sustainability conditions — it will effectively set the benchmark for regulated data centre capacity allocation across Southeast Asia. Other governments in the region are watching closely.

**JAMES:** Third: any progress on the NVIDIA Vera Rubin supply chain. The architecture is defined. The performance targets are public. The unknown is whether TSMC's advanced packaging ramp and SK Hynix's HBM4 production can reach the volumes needed for broad commercial deployment by late 2026 or whether the timeline slips into 2027. That timing has direct implications for the next wave of data centre construction specifications.

**MAYA:** And fourth: nuclear licensing timelines. Both the US and UK governments have stated commitments to accelerating small modular reactor approvals. If concrete regulatory milestones — site approvals, safety certifications — emerge in the next three to six months, it will meaningfully de-risk the Equinix-style nuclear portfolio strategies that are currently priced as long-duration options rather than near-term deliverables.
