## Segment 1 — The Grid Wall: Power Becomes the Binding Constraint

**MAYA:** In our first episode, we examined the convergence of power generation and digital infrastructure — the thesis that energy assets are becoming digital infrastructure assets. In Episode Two, we tracked the nuclear power purchase agreement wave from Meta, Microsoft, and Equinix, and the beginning of SMR programmes as board-level portfolio commitments. This week, that electricity story took on a sharper and more urgent dimension. Power is no longer one constraint among several. It has become the primary bottleneck in data centre deployment — ahead of capital, land, and chips.

**JAMES:** The numbers are precise and uncomfortable. Northern Virginia — which hosts the world's densest concentration of data centre capacity — has issued formal capacity warnings through 2028. New data centre permit applications have effectively halted in several counties. In Texas, the grid operator ERCOT is projecting data centre demand to add twenty-four gigawatts of new load by 2031 — pushing total system demand from eighty-seven gigawatts today to a hundred and forty-five gigawatts. That is a seventy percent increase in six years. Building reliable generation and transmission to match that growth typically takes a decade, not six years.

**MAYA:** And the grid interconnection queue delays are the most tangible expression of that problem. Data centre operators are reporting connection timelines of twenty-four to seventy-two months — meaning a project with capital secured, planning permissions granted, and hardware ordered can sit idle for up to six years waiting for a utility connection. PJM — the grid operator covering the mid-Atlantic and Midwest United States — recorded an eight-hundred-and-thirty-three percent surge in capacity auction clearing prices in 2024, driven almost entirely by data centre demand forecasts.

**JAMES:** When you combine that with Nadella's disclosure about idle GPU racks, something important becomes clear: the highest-returning AI infrastructure asset class on the planet is being constrained not by capital or chips, but by the national electricity grid. The bottleneck has migrated entirely to the physical layer.

**MAYA:** The read-through for European and Nordic investors is structural. The constraint that is freezing data centre investment in Northern Virginia and stalling ERCOT is precisely the constraint that does not exist in Scandinavia. Established grid connections, firm power purchase agreements, hydroelectric baseload, and offshore wind pipelines are not just energy credentials. They are, in the current environment, data centre location permits that markets like Virginia cannot issue right now. In Episode One, we described the Nordic position as a tailwind. At this point, it is a moat — and it is deepening with every month that US grid constraints extend.


## Segment 2 — The Hyperion JV: Infrastructure Finance Finds a New Architecture

**MAYA:** Against the backdrop of power scarcity, the most consequential single transaction of the past week was announced in Richland Parish, Louisiana. Meta has formed a twenty-seven-billion-dollar joint venture with Blue Owl Capital to fund what is on track to become the largest data centre on Earth.

**JAMES:** The numbers are difficult to absorb even with context. The Hyperion campus covers approximately three thousand six hundred and fifty acres in rural Louisiana — nearly twice the total land area of Central Park — and could ultimately accommodate five gigawatts of IT capacity. The first two-gigawatt phase targets completion by 2030. There are currently three thousand seven hundred construction workers on site, with the workforce projected to peak at five thousand by the middle of this year.

**MAYA:** And Meta is not just building a data centre. It is building the power generation to feed it. The company is funding the construction of seven new natural gas plants through Entergy, delivering five-point-two gigawatts of dedicated generation. Combined with existing facilities, that is ten gas plants and more than seven gigawatts of power serving a single AI campus. The carbon implications of that choice — and the question of how it sits alongside Meta's published climate commitments — are questions the company will need to answer directly in its sustainability disclosures.

**JAMES:** The Blue Owl JV is the financial engineering story here, and it has implications beyond this single transaction. The structure separates real estate ownership from operations. Meta sells the physical infrastructure into the joint venture and leases it back. That unlocks institutional capital — pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds — that wants the yield and security of real asset ownership without the operational risk of running AI infrastructure. It is the same logic as sale-leaseback structures that have long financed corporate real estate. The difference is the collateral: instead of office buildings, it is five gigawatts of AI compute capacity anchored by Meta's three-point-three billion monthly active users.

**MAYA:** In Episode Two, we discussed CoreWeave's investment-grade GPU loan as a milestone in the institutionalisation of AI compute financing. This transaction is that same impulse operating at the physical layer. The twenty-seven billion dollar figure is not the ceiling — it is the capital commitment for phase one. At five gigawatts of ultimate capacity, the total investment envelope for Hyperion likely exceeds sixty billion dollars. That makes it not just the largest data centre ever built. It may become the largest single infrastructure investment in American history.


## Segment 3 — AMD's $100 Billion Moment: Nvidia's Monopoly Tested at Scale

**MAYA:** We have spent considerable time across both prior episodes tracking NVIDIA's hardware roadmap — the Vera Rubin architecture, the HBM4 supply constraints, the GPU lead times running between thirty-six and fifty-two weeks. This week's compute story is the competitive response to all of that: a transaction that represents the most serious structural challenge to Nvidia's dominance of AI training silicon that the market has yet seen.

**JAMES:** On the twenty-fourth of February, AMD and Meta announced a multi-year partnership to deploy up to six gigawatts of AMD Instinct MI450 GPUs across Meta's global data centre fleet. The deal value exceeds one hundred billion dollars, making it the largest single GPU procurement agreement in recorded industry history. Meta has also been granted warrants for a hundred and sixty million AMD shares — approximately ten percent of the company — vesting against shipment and stock-price milestones. This is not a vendor-customer relationship. It is a structural partnership with equity alignment.

**MAYA:** For context: AMD's total revenue across all products in 2025 was approximately thirty billion dollars. The Meta deal alone would generate twenty to twenty-five billion dollars annually once fully ramped. And the strategic logic from Meta's side is straightforward: the company is spending a hundred to a hundred and thirty-five billion dollars in capital expenditure this year. At that scale, single-supplier dependence on Nvidia — with its thirty-six-to-fifty-two-week lead times and HBM4 memory constraints — is an unacceptable operational risk.

**JAMES:** For the broader GPU market, the significance is that AMD is no longer competing on benchmark comparisons or research paper metrics. It is competing on gigawatts of deployed capacity, which is the only metric that matters to hyperscalers operating at this scale. One gigawatt of AMD Instinct MI450 is deploying in the second half of 2026 — that is the real-world performance test. If those chips run within acceptable performance and reliability margins at gigawatt scale, it changes the procurement calculus for every major AI lab and hyperscaler. Nvidia's monopoly pricing power — which has been a central assumption in the equity market's valuation of the company — comes under genuine competitive pressure for the first time.

**MAYA:** And Nvidia's response is already visible. The company reportedly skipped consumer gaming GPU releases in 2026 to prioritise AI chip production. It has locked in approximately fifty percent of TSMC's advanced packaging capacity for 2026. And the AWS supply deal we covered in Episode Two — one million Rubin GPUs delivered between 2026 and 2027 — looks, in part, like a defensive move to cement hyperscaler relationships before AMD reaches deployment velocity. The competition is real, and it is now measured in gigawatts.


## Segment 4 — Equinix and CPP Buy atNorth: The Nordic Premium Has a Price

**MAYA:** We have described the Nordic infrastructure advantage across two episodes — the renewable energy, the natural cooling, the political stability, the absence of grid constraints. This week, that thesis received its most direct market validation: a transaction with a specific enterprise value attached to it. On the twenty-seventh of February, Equinix and Canada Pension Plan Investment Board jointly announced the acquisition of atNorth — the high-density Nordic colocation operator — for four-point-two billion dollars.

**JAMES:** atNorth operates eight data centres across Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden. It has secured one gigawatt of power capacity across those facilities, and its infrastructure is specifically engineered for liquid-cooled, high-density AI workloads — the format that is becoming the industry standard as GPU rack densities increase beyond what air cooling can manage.

**MAYA:** The capital structure of this acquisition is as significant as the price. CPP Investments — one of the world's largest pension funds, with more than six hundred billion dollars in total assets — takes approximately sixty percent of the equity, representing around one-point-six billion dollars of committed capital. Equinix takes the remaining forty percent and provides operational expertise. That configuration — a global colocation operator as co-investor and operator, alongside a pension fund as the anchor capital provider — is the model that will repeat across this asset class.

**JAMES:** When Canada Pension Plan commits one-point-six billion dollars of equity to Nordic AI colocation, it is pricing that infrastructure with a twenty-to-thirty year investment horizon. The question CPP has answered — implicitly, through this transaction — is: where will AI compute capacity be in demand across a full institutional investment cycle? Its answer is Northern Europe, where power, cooling, political stability, and regulatory predictability converge in a combination that no other region on Earth currently replicates.

**MAYA:** The valuation per gigawatt of secured capacity implied by this deal is materially higher than comparable colocation assets in power-constrained US markets. The Nordic premium — which in Episode One we described as a planning preference becoming a fiduciary obligation — has crystallised into a transaction price. Four-point-two billion dollars for one gigawatt of high-density Nordic capacity is the data point that boards and investment committees across Europe and North America will now use as their reference.


## Segment 5 — ASEAN's Data Corridors and Amazon's European Bet

**MAYA:** Two final developments that extend the geopolitical dimension. The first is in Southeast Asia, building directly on the Malaysia and Singapore story from Episode Two. In January 2026, ASEAN Digital Ministers formally endorsed the ASEAN Framework on Cross-Border Cloud Computing — and with it, the concept of Trusted Data Corridors. This is the most significant regional data governance shift in Southeast Asia in years, and its implications for infrastructure investment are direct.

**JAMES:** The mechanism matters precisely because it addresses a genuine friction point. A Trusted Data Corridor allows free data flow between accredited data centres in member states with comparable data protection regimes — without requiring the standard contractual clauses or bilateral data transfer agreements that currently slow and complicate multi-country cloud operations. For an operator building regional infrastructure across ten ASEAN jurisdictions, that is a meaningful reduction in compliance cost and legal overhead.

**MAYA:** The ASEAN cloud market is projected at forty-three billion dollars by 2030. The framework directly reduces the regulatory friction that has made Southeast Asia's fragmented governance environment a disincentive for regional-scale investment. The near-term beneficiaries are the markets that already have the most data centre momentum — Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand — as investment that previously concentrated in Singapore, constrained by power and land, can now spread across the corridor without regulatory penalty. In Episode Two, we described Malaysia as already accounting for more than half of under-construction data centre capacity in the region. The Trusted Data Corridor framework adds a regulatory tailwind to the physical infrastructure story.

**JAMES:** And in Europe, Amazon's commitment to Spain deserves attention as the clearest current expression of how hyperscalers are thinking about European sovereign cloud positioning. Amazon has now committed thirty-three-point-seven billion euros in total investment in Spain — eighteen billion euros in new commitment, added to a prior fifteen-point-seven billion euro pledge. The scope spans data centres across three Spanish provinces, a server manufacturing plant, logistics infrastructure, and a hundred solar and wind projects. Spain is becoming an AI infrastructure hub.

**MAYA:** The strategic driver is data sovereignty regulation. European frameworks — GDPR, the Data Act, the AI Act — require significant categories of data to be processed within European borders, by entities subject to European jurisdiction. As that regulatory perimeter hardens, the competitive advantage accumulates with the hyperscalers who have invested most deeply in European physical infrastructure. Amazon's thirty-three-point-seven billion euro Spain commitment is, in that frame, not primarily a bet on Spanish consumer demand. It is a bet on the durability of European data localisation requirements — and on the value of first-mover advantage in capturing the regulatory moat they create.


## What to Watch — Four Decisions Ahead

**JAMES:** Four things to monitor in the weeks ahead. First: Louisiana utility commission approvals for Meta's seven new natural gas plants at Hyperion. That regulatory process is the critical path for the campus's power delivery timeline. Any friction there will test Meta's ability to manage construction complexity at unprecedented scale — and will surface the tension between its AI infrastructure ambitions and its climate commitments.

**MAYA:** Second: AMD's first MI450 shipments to Meta in the second half of 2026. That is the real-world performance test that will determine whether the hundred-billion-dollar deal remains structurally intact or faces renegotiation. If AMD's chips run within acceptable performance and reliability margins at gigawatt scale, it forces every major AI lab and hyperscaler to reconsider their Nvidia dependency assumptions.

**JAMES:** Third: PJM's next capacity auction results. The previous auction cleared at eight hundred and thirty-three percent above prior levels. If that repricing is becoming the new normal, data centre operating cost projections for the US East Coast will require significant revision — which in turn makes European alternatives, with more predictable long-term power pricing, more attractive in institutional portfolio comparisons.

**MAYA:** And fourth: the first ASEAN Trusted Data Corridor certifications. The framework is endorsed in principle. Operationalisation — which regulators in which countries issue accreditations to which data centres — will determine whether this translates into real investment flows or remains an aspirational policy commitment. The first accreditations are expected from Malaysia and Singapore, and they will be watched closely by every operator making regional infrastructure decisions in 2026 and 2027.

**MAYA:** The word connecting this week's stories is constraint. The grid constraint is redirecting trillions of dollars of data centre investment toward markets that have power — and hardening the Nordic and European advantage we have traced from our first episode. The GPU supply constraint is creating structural space for AMD to compete at a scale that was unimaginable eighteen months ago. The capital structure constraint — the need to finance data centres at a speed that outpaces hyperscaler balance sheets — is producing new joint venture architectures and drawing institutional investors into colocation M&A. And regulatory constraints in Europe and Southeast Asia are solidifying into competitive moats for operators who have positioned early.

**JAMES:** Constraint, in infrastructure investing, is not the opposite of opportunity. It is the precondition for it. The assets that become most valuable in this cycle are those that resolve a genuine constraint — a grid connection in a power-scarce market, a colocation footprint in a regulatory-predictable jurisdiction, a GPU supply deal that reduces dependence on a single dominant supplier. The question for every investor in this space is not where is the capital flowing — that is now visible to everyone. The question is: which constraints are most durable, and who owns the assets that resolve them?
